First-Order Circuits Review

First-order circuits are electrical circuits that can be reduced to only one energy storage element — either a capacitor or an inductor — along with resistors and independent sources.

Types of First-Order Circuits

  • RC Circuit – Resistor and Capacitor
  • RL Circuit – Resistor and Inductor

Natural Response

Occurs when no external sources are present (e.g., discharging a capacitor).

\[ x(t) = x(0) e^{-t/\tau} \]

where \( \tau \) is the time constant:

  • \( \tau = RC \) for RC circuits
  • \( \tau = \frac{L}{R} \) for RL circuits

Step Response

When a source is applied at \( t = 0 \), the output approaches a final steady-state value:

\[ x(t) = x_{\infty} + \left( x(0) - x_{\infty} \right) e^{-t/\tau} \]

Key Points to Remember

  • The rate of change is exponential.
  • Time constant \( \tau \) defines how fast the circuit responds.
  • At \( t = \tau \): 63.2% of total change has occurred.
  • At \( t = 5\tau \): system is considered to be at steady state.

Example: RC Charging Circuit

Given a series RC circuit connected to a DC source at \( t = 0 \):

\[ V_c(t) = V_s \left( 1 - e^{-t/RC} \right) \]

The voltage across the capacitor starts at 0 and asymptotically approaches \( V_s \).

Example: RL Circuit with Step Input

Given a series RL circuit with a step voltage source:

\[ i(t) = \frac{V_s}{R} \left( 1 - e^{-tR/L} \right) \]

The inductor current starts at 0 and ramps up toward \( \frac{V_s}{R} \).
