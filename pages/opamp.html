What is an Operational Amplifier?

Operational amplifiers — or op-amps — are some of the most important components in all of Electrical Engineering. They can be configured in endless ways to perform mathematical operations like addition, subtraction, integration, differentiation, filtering, and even voltage regulation.

Golden Rules of Ideal Op-Amps

  • Infinite open-loop gain: \( A_v = \infty \)
  • Infinite input impedance: \( i^+ = i^- = 0 \)
  • Zero output impedance
  • Zero offset voltage: \( V^+ = V^- \)
  • Infinite bandwidth

Of course, real op-amps aren’t perfect — they have non-idealities like input bias currents, offset voltages, limited bandwidth, and slew rate. But even with those limitations, they’re powerful tools in both analog and mixed-signal design.

Common Op-Amp Configurations

  • Inverting / Non-inverting Amplifiers
  • Integrators and Differentiators
  • Comparators
  • Filters (Lowpass / Highpass / Bandpass)

Integrator

Output voltage: \\[ V_{out}(t) = -\frac{1}{RC} \int_0^t V_{in}(\tau)\, d\tau \\]

Differentiator

Output voltage: \\[ V_{out}(t) = -RC \frac{dV_{in}(t)}{dt} \\]

Comparator

Ideal output: \\[ V_{out} = \begin{cases} V_{CC} & \text{if } V_+ > V_- \\\\ 0 & \text{if } V_+ \leq V_- \end{cases} \\]

Low-Pass Filter

Transfer function: \\[ H(s) = \frac{1}{1 + sRC} \\]

High-Pass Filter

Transfer function: \\[ H(s) = \frac{sRC}{1 + sRC} \\]
